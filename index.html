History and Discovery

The Origin of a Revolution

The path toward X-ray diffraction began at the end of the 19th century and the beginning of the 20th. It was not a single discovery, but a chain of brilliance that allowed "seeing" for the first time the atomic structure of materials.

Fundamental Actors

  • 1895 - Wilhelm Röntgen: Discovered X-rays, although initially he did not know they could be used to study crystals.
  • 1912 - Max von Laue: Formulated the hypothesis that if atoms were ordered within a crystal, they would function as a diffraction grating for X-rays. This experiment confirmed both the wave nature of X-rays and the atomic structure of crystals.
  • 1913 - The Braggs (Father and Son): William Henry and William Lawrence Bragg simplified von Laue's complex model by establishing Bragg's Law, which enables the calculation of actual atomic distances.

Fundamentals of Physics

The technique takes advantage of the fact that the wavelength of X-rays is comparable to the space between atoms in a crystal. When X-rays interact with electrons, an elastic scattering process occurs. The constructive interference of these waves, described by Bragg's law, allows obtaining information about the ordered arrangement of atoms

Biological and Chemical Sciences

The Structure of Life

In biology, X-ray diffraction (XRD) was the critical key to resolving the mystery of DNA. By crystallizing biological molecules, scientists can determine the precise position of every atom.

Phase Differentiation

Chemically, XRD can distinguish between substances with identical compositions but different structures (polymorphism). For example, it can differentiate between graphite and diamond, both composed solely of carbon but possessing radically different properties due to their atomic arrangement.

Physics and Mathematical Foundations

For diffraction to occur, the radiation's wavelength must be comparable to the distance between atoms.

Interplanar Distance Formula ($d$)

\( d_{hkl} = \frac{a}{\sqrt{h^2 + k^2 + l^2}} \)

This formula relates the spacing d between parallel crystalline planes with the Miller indices (h, k, l) and the lattice parameter a in cubic systems.

Bragg's Law

It is the mathematical foundation explaining how rays scattered by atoms reinforce each other (constructive interference).

\( n\lambda = 2d \sin(\theta) \)
  • n: Reflection order (integer).
  • λ: X-ray wavelength.
  • d: Distance between atomic planes.
  • θ: Angle of incidence.

Law of Sines

Used in molecular geometry calculations.

\( \frac{a}{\sin(A)} = \frac{b}{\sin(B)} = \frac{c}{\sin(C)} \)

Helps determine bond angles and distances in 3D space.

Law of Cosines

Essential for calculating distances in non-right triangles.

\( c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab \cos(C) \)

Used to find the length of bonds when angles are known.

Constructive Interference

Only when Bragg's Law is satisfied are the waves exiting the crystal "in phase," producing a measurable intensity peak. If it is not met, the waves cancel each other out.

Scientific Note: X-rays are utilized because their wavelength is sufficiently small to interact with the atomic spacing.

Technological Contributions and Applications

XRD has not only been fundamental to basic science but has also driven technological advancements across various fields.

Medicine

In medicine, XRD is employed to determine the structure of proteins and enzymes, which is essential for drug design. For instance, the structure of the HIV virus was resolved using this technique, facilitating the development of effective treatments.

Materials Engineering

In materials engineering, XRD assists in characterizing new materials such as superconductors and semiconductors, enabling the optimization of their properties for specific applications.
